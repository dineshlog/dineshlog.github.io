Core concept

The entitlement model at a glance

A tenant contains catalogs. Catalogs contain resources and access packages. Access packages combine resource roles with policies that govern request, approval, assignment, expiration, and review.

Tabbed details

Key objects and what they control

Use these as UI cards, modal panels, or glossary entries in a training portal.

Catalog

A catalog is the administrative container that groups related resources and access packages. It is a natural boundary for delegation and ownership.

PurposeOrganize resources and packages by department, function, project, or sensitivity.
DelegationCatalog owners and managers can maintain access packages without requiring global admin involvement.
Design hintAlign catalogs with business ownership boundaries instead of technical platform boundaries.
ExamplesFinance Catalog, Development Catalog, External Partner Catalog, Security Operations Catalog.

Access Package

An access package is the governed product that users request or receive. It bundles resource roles and the policies that control lifecycle.

ContainsGroups, Teams, applications, SharePoint sites, roles, permissions, and the role level granted on each.
Business mappingShould represent a job role, project role, partner role, or temporary access need.
User visibilityCan be visible in My Access or hidden and shared only through a direct package link.
Design hintKeep packages purpose-specific; avoid giant packages that become difficult to review.

Policy

Policies define who can request access, whether approval is required, what justifications are captured, and when access expires.

Requestor scopeInternal users, selected groups, all members, existing guests, external connected organizations, or admin-only assignment.
ApprovalSingle-stage or multi-stage approval with manager, selected approvers, sponsors, or resource owners.
LifecycleNever expires, expires after a number of days, or expires on a specific date.
ExtensionUsers may request an extension, optionally requiring approval and justification.

Assignment

An assignment is the active grant of the package to a user. It represents the operational state where resource access is provisioned.

Starts whenA request is approved, or an admin directly assigns the package.
EffectsUser receives the selected resource roles across the package resources.
Ends whenAccess expires, a review denies continuation, an admin removes assignment, or the package policy changes.
Risk reducedAssignments are easier to track than scattered group and application changes.

Access Review

Access reviews ask whether a user still needs a package. They help reduce stale access and provide an audit trail.

Reviewer optionsManager, resource owner, selected reviewer, self-review, or business sponsor.
FrequencyCommon cadences include monthly, quarterly, semiannual, or project-end review.
OutcomeContinue, remove, or apply default decision if reviewers do not respond.
Governance valueCreates defensible evidence that access is periodically verified.

Pain points addressed

What entitlement management improves

Use this section to explain why the model matters to security, compliance, operations, and the business.

+

Access sprawl

Packages replace scattered, one-off grants with intentional bundles tied to roles, projects, and business needs.

T

Stale access

Expiration, extensions, and access reviews reduce the risk of users retaining access after the need ends.

A

Weak accountability

Approvers, reviewers, and catalog owners create clear accountability for who granted access and why.

G

External collaboration risk

Partner and guest access can be time-bound, sponsor-driven, and reviewed instead of becoming permanent by default.

R

Manual repetition

Common onboarding patterns become reusable access packages instead of repeated tickets and manual group changes.

E

Audit gaps

Requests, approvals, justifications, assignments, and reviews provide a stronger evidence trail.

Tradeoffs

Pros and cons

Entitlement Management is powerful, but it works best with deliberate design, naming, ownership, and review practices.

Pros

  • YAutomates repeatable access requests and reduces manual ticket work.
  • YCombines multiple resource grants into one business-friendly package.
  • YSupports approvals, justifications, expiration, extensions, and access reviews.
  • YImproves external user governance with scoped, temporary access.
  • YEnables delegation to business owners while keeping a governance framework.

Cons / Watchouts

  • !Poor package naming or vague descriptions can confuse requestors and approvers.
  • !Overly broad packages can recreate access sprawl in a more polished form.
  • !Requires clear ownership; unmanaged catalogs can become stale.
  • !Some resource types and role scenarios may have feature limitations or preview status.
  • !Lifecycle settings need policy discipline; "never expires" should be used intentionally.

Best-practice design principle

Design each access package around a specific business outcome: a project role, partner role, application role, department function, or temporary access scenario. Then add the minimum resources, the right approvers, an expiration rule, and an access review cadence.
